Executive Summary

Digital commerce is entering a structural transition. Systems designed around search, ranking, and paid visibility are increasingly misaligned with how people want to buy and how businesses want to sell.

VIA addresses this mismatch by enabling agentic commerce. Instead of optimising for attention, VIA enables intent-first interaction, where autonomous and semi-autonomous agents act on behalf of buyers and sellers to discover, negotiate, and coordinate transactions.

VIA is designed to operate across the AI assistants people already use, functioning as neutral coordination infrastructure rather than a marketplace, advertising platform, or closed ecosystem.

The Problem

Most digital commerce systems are built around being seen. Businesses pay to compete for impressions, ranking, and reach, regardless of whether the audience has any realistic intent to purchase.

For consumers, this results in overwhelming results, persistent advertising, and increasing effort to reach confident decisions. For businesses, it results in high acquisition costs, wasted spend, and limited visibility into genuine demand.

The system rewards volume and visibility rather than relevance and readiness.

The Shift to Agentic Commerce

Digital commerce has progressed from search-led discovery to social-led influence. The next phase is agent-mediated execution.

Instead of browsing and comparing manually, users express intent in everyday language. Agents translate that intent into structured requirements, engage relevant supply, and return outcomes that can be approved, refined, or executed.

The user shifts from managing complexity to setting boundaries and making final decisions.

What Makes Commerce Agentic

Agentic commerce is defined by execution rather than conversation.

An agentic system can capture intent, translate it into constraints, evaluate and negotiate supply, execute transactions within defined authority, and manage post-purchase actions.

The defining characteristic is autonomy under guardrails. Agents act, but only within limits defined by users or merchants.

Adoption Expectations

Agentic commerce will be adopted progressively. Low-risk and repeat purchases will be delegated first, followed by draft-and-approve flows for higher-consideration decisions.

Adoption depends on transparency, reversibility, and control. Users must understand why actions were taken and be able to adjust or undo them easily.

System Architecture

VIA is an intent-driven, agent-native commerce coordination system composed of four core layers.

  • Intent and Interface Layer
  • Agent Identity and Trust
  • Messaging and Discovery
  • Economic Settlement

VIA captures intent before it becomes a transaction and routes it through a neutral, protocol-driven coordination framework.

The VIA Coordination Framework

VIA aligns intent schemas, agent trust rules, messaging conventions, and agentic payment logic without centralising control.

Discovery is routed rather than broadcast. Trust is accumulated through observable behaviour rather than inherited from platforms.

VIA is designed to interoperate with existing commerce infrastructure rather than replace it.

FA$H as a Trust Enhancement Layer

FA$H is an existing ERC-20 token intended to support trust-related mechanisms as agentic commerce matures.

Potential applications include agent credibility signalling, participation thresholds, and economic backstops for dispute resolution, reinforcing trust through observable commitment rather than reputation alone.

Conclusion

Agentic commerce represents a rebalancing of effort, trust, and control in digital markets. It does not remove choice, but changes who processes it.

VIA is designed to support this transition by enabling intent-first coordination without opacity, lock-in, or dependency on attention-based models.
